Pelagic ecosystems are dynamic, data-rich environments that require robust instrumentation, adaptive workflows, and interdisciplinary collaboration. My work in pelagic imaging focuses on the deployment, operation, processing, and interpretation of imaging systems used to observe biological and physical processes in the water column. These efforts support oceanographic research, ecosystem monitoring, and autonomous observing systems operating across Arctic and sub-Arctic marine environments.

## Capabilities

### Deployment Support & Field Operations

I support the integration and deployment of pelagic imaging systems across a variety of research platforms, including ships, moorings, autonomous vehicles, and uncrewed systems. This work includes:

- Sensor and platform integration
- Mission planning and field logistics
- Real-time system monitoring and troubleshooting
- Instrument calibration

Field operations often require balancing scientific objectives with environmental constraints, bandwidth limitations, and platform-specific requirements. My role bridges engineering, data systems, and scientific operations to ensure reliable acquisition of high-quality imaging data.

---

### Real-Time Data Processing

Many modern imaging systems generate data continuously during deployment. I develop and maintain workflows that enable:

- Automated ingestion and quality control
- Real-time image classification and object detection
- Telemetry-aware processing pipelines
- Edge computing workflows for low-bandwidth platforms
- Rapid situational awareness products for field teams

These systems help researchers make informed operational decisions while deployments are underway, particularly in remote or autonomous observing environments.

---

### Post-Deployment Data Processing

Following recovery, pelagic imaging datasets often require substantial processing before scientific analysis can begin. My work includes:

- Image archive management and metadata standardization
- Batch processing and annotation workflows
- Object segmentation and feature extraction
- Data harmonization across sensors and missions
- Reproducible processing pipelines using open-source tools

The goal is to transform large, heterogeneous imaging datasets into accessible scientific products that support ecological interpretation and long-term monitoring.

---

### Machine Learning & Computer Vision

Machine learning is increasingly central to pelagic imaging workflows. I apply computer vision and AI techniques to support automated analysis of marine imagery, including:

- Plankton and particle classification
- Object detection and tracking
- Semi-supervised annotation workflows
- Model training and validation
- Deployment of inference pipelines on autonomous systems

These approaches reduce manual processing requirements while enabling scalable analysis of increasingly large imaging datasets.

---

### Training & Capacity Building

Effective imaging programs depend on accessible workflows and knowledgeable operators. I provide technical training and collaborative support for:

- Instrument operations and deployment procedures
- Imaging data management
- Machine learning workflow development
- Reproducible scientific computing practices
- Open-source software and research infrastructure

Training efforts are tailored to interdisciplinary teams that include scientists, engineers, students, and field technicians.

# Selected Projects

## In Situ Ichthyoplankton Imaging System – Dual-Purpose Imaging (ISIIS-DPI)

I have contributed to work involving the **ISIIS-DPI** system led by **[Russ Hopcroft (University of Alaska Fairbanks)](https://www.uaf.edu/cfos/people/faculty/detail/russell-hopcroft.php)**. ISIIS is a towed in situ imaging platform designed to capture high-resolution observations of planktonic organisms and fine-scale oceanographic structure across broad spatial scales.

Contributions have included support for imaging workflows, data processing infrastructure, and operational coordination associated with deployment and analysis activities ([*see* Kepner thesis](https://scholarworks.alaska.edu/uaf_grad_oceanography/43/)). This work supports ecosystem research focused on Arctic and sub-Arctic marine environments, including zooplankton community structure and physical-biological interactions.

- [Bellamare ISIIS-DPI System](https://bellamare-us.com/isiis-dpi/)

## Autonomous Pelagic Imaging with Uncrewed Systems (UxS)

I have also participated in the development and operation of pelagic imaging workflows associated with autonomous and uncrewed systems, including glider-based observing platforms operated in collaboration with:

- Seth Danielson
- Hank S.
- John Horn

These efforts explore the integration of imaging and environmental sensing capabilities into autonomous platforms capable of long-duration operations in remote marine environments. Work has included support for onboard processing concepts, telemetry-aware workflows, and post-mission data integration.

Autonomous observing systems expand the spatial and temporal reach of pelagic imaging while enabling new approaches to ecosystem monitoring in challenging environments such as the Arctic.

---

# Approach

Pelagic imaging sits at the intersection of oceanography, engineering, data science, and artificial intelligence. My approach emphasizes:

- Operational reliability in harsh environments
- Open and reproducible workflows
- Scalable data processing infrastructure
- Collaborative interdisciplinary development
- Practical deployment-focused solutions

I am particularly interested in building systems that move imaging workflows closer to real-time ecological understanding through automation, machine learning, and autonomous sensing platforms.
