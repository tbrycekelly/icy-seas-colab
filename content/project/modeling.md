Our work focuses on the integration of environmental observation, numerical modeling, and scientific computing to better understand complex marine systems. Across projects in oceanography, ecosystem dynamics, and autonomous observing systems, we are interested in how computation can bridge scales between raw environmental measurements and interpretable models of the physical world.

This work spans biogeochemical modeling, satellite and in situ data integration, machine learning, high-performance computing, and emerging approaches to field-deployable computational systems.

At its core, my research is motivated by a central question:

> How can we build computational systems that allow us to observe, model, and interpret dynamic environmental processes in increasingly adaptive, autonomous, and scalable ways?

---

# Research Themes

## Integrating Observation & Modeling

Modern environmental science depends on combining heterogeneous observations with physically grounded models. Many of the systems we work on integrate:

- Satellite remote sensing
- Shipboard observations
- Autonomous platforms
- Imaging systems
- Numerical circulation models
- Machine learning workflows
- Real-time environmental telemetry

These systems generate large, fragmented, and highly dynamic datasets that require substantial computational infrastructure to transform into coherent scientific understanding.

Our work focuses on designing workflows that connect these observational and modeling domains together in operationally useful ways.

---

## Biogeochemical Dynamics Across Scales

A major focus of our work involves understanding how physical transport processes influence marine ecosystem structure and biogeochemical cycling.

This includes:

- Nutrient transport and export
- Physical-biological coupling
- Cross-shelf and lateral advection
- Ecosystem variability
- Remote sensing interpretation
- Data-constrained environmental analysis

These questions often require combining sparse in situ observations with larger-scale satellite products and numerical models to resolve processes that cannot be directly observed from a single platform alone.

# Featured Research

## Lateral Advection Supports Nitrogen Export in the Oligotrophic Open-Ocean Gulf of Mexico

One example of this work is research examining nitrogen export dynamics in the oligotrophic Gulf of Mexico (see [details here](https://icyseascolab.io/publication/2021kelly/)).

This project integrated:

- In situ nutrient and productivity observations
- Satellite-derived environmental products
- Physical transport analysis
- Biogeochemical interpretation
- Cross-scale ecosystem modeling

The work demonstrated how lateral transport processes can sustain export production in nutrient-limited open-ocean systems, highlighting the importance of integrating physical circulation with biological and chemical observations.

More broadly, this project reflects our interest in computational approaches that synthesize multiple observational and modeling domains into unified environmental interpretations.

The project reinforced the importance of scalable computational workflows capable of integrating environmental data across broad spatial domains while maintaining biologically meaningful interpretation.

# Scientific Computing as Environmental Infrastructure

A central aspect of our work is the idea that scientific computing is not simply an analytical backend, but a core component of modern environmental observing systems.

Increasingly, environmental science depends on computational pipelines that can:

- Assimilate heterogeneous datasets
- Operate across distributed systems
- Support real-time analysis
- Enable autonomous decision-making
- Scale from embedded devices to HPC environments
- Integrate physically based and data-driven models

Our interests therefore extend beyond traditional modeling toward the computational architectures that support environmental understanding itself.

---

# Technical Computing Interests

## High-Performance & Distributed Computing

Many environmental datasets now exceed the scale of traditional desktop workflows. We develop and operate computational systems for:

- Parallel scientific analysis
- GPU acceleration
- Distributed processing
- Reproducible computational workflows
- Large-scale environmental data integration
- Scientific software engineering

We are particularly interested in practical systems that bridge research computing with operational environmental sensing.

---

## Edge & Field-Deployable Computing

An increasingly important area of interest involves pushing computational capability directly into field systems and autonomous platforms.

This includes work involving:

- Low-power GPU systems
- Single-board computers (SBCs)
- Edge AI workflows
- Telemetry-aware computation
- Autonomous sensing systems
- Embedded scientific instrumentation

We are interested in how these systems can support adaptive observing strategies, onboard inference, and real-time environmental interpretation in remote environments where bandwidth and power are constrained.

This direction connects closely with our work in pelagic imaging and autonomous ocean observing systems, where the distinction between sensor, model, and computational platform is becoming increasingly blurred.

---

## Machine Learning & Hybrid Environmental Modeling

Machine learning provides powerful tools for interpreting large environmental datasets, but we are especially interested in approaches that remain physically interpretable and scientifically grounded.

Current interests include:

- Physics-informed machine learning
- Hybrid model + ML systems
- Environmental classification
- Image and sensor fusion
- Spatiotemporal analysis
- Autonomous inference systems

Rather than replacing physical understanding, these approaches can augment traditional modeling by helping identify structure, variability, and emergent behavior within increasingly complex datasets.

---

# Research Direction

Across these projects, our broader goal is to develop computational systems that improve our ability to observe and understand dynamic environmental processes across scales.

This includes exploring how:

- Autonomous systems
- Distributed sensing
- Edge computation
- GPU acceleration
- Machine learning
- Numerical modeling
- Real-time telemetry
- Environmental imaging

can be integrated into cohesive scientific workflows capable of operating in remote and data-limited environments.

We are particularly interested in systems that move environmental analysis closer to the point of observation itself — enabling adaptive, computationally aware sensing systems that can operate in real time within the physical world they are attempting to model.
